Step 1: Simulation

Simulation has three parts

  • Physical model
  • Physiological model
  • Psychological model

Step 1: Simulation - Physical Model

  • Model of the bicycle
  • Slipstream model (fluid dynamics)

Step 1: Simulation - Bicyle model

The Bicycle model we used takes into account

  • Aerodynamic drag
  • Kinetic energy

Step 1: Simulation - Assumptions

We made some assumptions about the model:

  • Perfectly flat race track
  • No losses of energy due to friction.

Step 1: Simulation - Slipstream Model

A Slipstream is the low pressure zone in the wake of a moving object.

We correct for this by multiplying the aerodynamic drag by a correction factor:

$$ 0.62 - 0.0104 \cdot d_w + 0.0452 \cdot d_w^2 $$

Step 1: Simulation - Physiological Model

There are two "modes" in which the human muscle can work.

  • aerobic
  • anaerobic

To describe the physical attributes of the cyclist, we use two variables:

  • $P_{max}$, the maximum power output of the cyclist.
  • $P_{CP}$, the maximum aerobic power of the cyclist.

Step 1: Simulation - Psychological Model

Each cyclist can have his own strategy, the code is designed so that these strategies can easily added.

The model we use limits the decisions to:

  • Break-away
  • Follow a team mate in a break-away.
  • Cooperate with your pack.

We implemented a simple probabilistic model.

Step 2: Game Theory - Model

Pay-off dependencies

Probability of finishing first depends on

$$CS_{i,n} = \frac{P_{ped_{i,n}} \cdot \delta^{(n)}}{E_{i,n} \cdot v},\ i\in\{1,2\}$$

Advantage from drafting:

$$F_{draft}(x) = (0.62 - 0.0104 \cdot d_w + 0.0452 \cdot d_w^2)\cdot (1/2)\cdot c_d\cdot \rho\cdot A\cdot x^2$$ where $x$ is the speed of the player

Step 2: Game Theory - Model

Both players cooperate

  • Players choose to help each other
  • The advantage will tend to go to the player that is second
  • Pay-off for the player who is second is: $$(1-P)\cdot \frac{F_{draft}(s)}{F_{max}} + 1$$
